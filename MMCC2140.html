Major Essay 2021 S1

Fan Fiction and AO3

The enforcement of copyright laws and strict censorship of content for the transformative and creative work community (fan fiction/fandom community) lead creators the need to create and move to site such as Archive of Our Own (AO3) to defend their fair use (fair dealing) of media content (Star Track, Harry Potter) (Lothian, 2011, p.3). AO3 and other sites like it allows creators and consumers to share and consume fan work free from legal repercussions, they operate in the grey area between copyright law and fair dealing (or fair use in the US) (Fathallah, 2020, p.858). Members of remix communities follow many social norms and behaviors primarily ‘copyright norms’ to protect their works and to protect the community (Fiesler & Bruckman, 2019, p3-4). The fandom community is an example of a disciplinary society, however the institutional surveillance of content on certain sites pushes members away while also bonding the community together against the common enemy of copyright laws and web site policy. The read/write culture’s (Lessig, L. 2008, p.28) of the fandom community are protected by community’s copyright norms practices on sites like AO3. The members behave like keen’s noble amateurs (Keen, A. 2008, pp. 59) fighting against expert’s view of copyright against their own view on copyright. The control of copyright and the control of social norms help shapes the land scape and freedom of fandom creators and sites like AO3 help protect and promote creators’ freedom. Whether freedom from prosecution or freedom to remix media content AO3 is a safe space for all members of the fan fiction community.

The history of fandom (fan fiction, transformative work, remix culture etc) is long and started long before the internet existed but its story is intertwined with copyright and those that want to control it. Websites that hosted fan work have tried to control and censor the content for example, FanFiction.net’s cracked down on adult content in 2003, LiveJournal purchased by a Russian company in late 2007 had “crack down on content that might be illegal or obscene”, ended up deleting many journals (Schwedel, H. What were the specific things that drove people away from LiveJournal? para.1) and Tumbler’s band on not safe for work (NSFW) content (porn) in 2017 (Radulovic, P., 2018 para.1). Like the pirates that theorist Mason, M. talks about in ‘The Pirate’s Dilemma’ (Mason, M. 2008) the “crack down” forces the community to boycott these sites and migrates to other sites such as Archive of Our Own (AO3) and twitter. The Organization for Transformative Work (OTW) formed in 2008 (Lothian, A. 2011, Screenshot, AO3 front page para.1) in response to the purge and restrictions to fan work and Archive of Our Own (AO3) is a project they launched to give creators a reliable place in the “changing world of publishing and property” (Lothian, A. 2011, 2. Ownership, para. 1).
